\(\Rightarrow\) Analysis of Gear
Pressure angle (Video 1), (Video 2) The angle between the line of action and a line tangent to the pitch circle of the gear.
Sliding velocity The velocity at which one gear tooth slides past another during their meshing. \(V_{sliding} = PQ(\omega_1+\omega_2\)
Length of contact The axial distance along the line of action over which the gears are in contact. Length of contact (\(L_c\)) = KP+PL
\(KP = \sqrt{R_A-R^2cos^2 \phi} - Rsin \phi\)

\(PL = \sqrt{r_A-r^2cos^2 \phi} - rsin \phi\)
Arc of contact The radial distance along the pitch circle through which the gears are in contact. Arc of contact (\(A_c\)) = \(L_c \cos \phi\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Classifications of gear trains
Simple Gear Train (Image) In a simple gear train, two or more gears are arranged in a series, with each gear meshing with its adjacent gear. The input and output shafts are located at the ends of the gear train.
Compound Gear train (Image) A compound gear train consists of two or more simple gear trains connected in series. This arrangement allows for greater flexibility in changing the speed and torque characteristics of the system.
Reverted Gear Train (Image) In a reverted gear train, the input and output shafts are parallel and on the same side of the gear train. This arrangement is commonly used when the input and output shafts need to rotate in the same direction.
Epicyclic Gear Train (Image) Epicyclic gear trains, also known as planetary gear trains, involve one or more gears that rotate around a central gear. They offer compact and versatile configurations and find applications in various systems like automotive transmissions.

\(\Rightarrow\) Gyroscopic precession A phenomenon that occurs when a spinning object experiences a torque that causes its axis of rotation to change. It is a result of the conservation of angular momentum and the interaction between the spinning object and an applied external force. \(T = I\omega \omega_p\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Classification of Governors (Video) Governors are devices used in engine control systems to regulate the speed of the engine. They are classified based on various factors such as control mechanism, type of energy used, and type of operation. \(\Rightarrow\) Grashof's Law Grashof's law is used to determine the mobility of mechanisms based on the lengths of the links and the type of joints used in the mechanism. It states that for a planar four-bar linkage, the sum of the shortest and longest links must be less than or equal to the sum of the remaining two links for the mechanism to have mobility. \(s+l\le p+q\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Velocity Analysis of Mechanisms
I-centre method (Video 1) (Video 2) The I-Center method is a graphical technique used for velocity analysis of mechanisms. It involves constructing I-Centers, which are instantaneous centers of zero velocity, to determine the velocities of different points in the mechanism.
Kennedy's theorem (Video) Kennedy's theorem is a method used for velocity analysis of mechanisms. It states that if three points on a rigid body have known velocities, the velocity of any other point on the body can be determined by constructing a triangle of velocities.
Angular Velocity Method (Video) The Angular Velocity Method is a technique used in the analysis of mechanisms. It involves determining the angular velocities of different links or points in the mechanism to study their motion and behavior. Between link m and n:
\(\omega_m(I_{1m}.I_{mn})=\omega_n(I_{1n}.I_{mn})\)
Relative Velocity Method (Video) The Relative Velocity Method is a technique used in the analysis of mechanisms. It involves analyzing the relative velocities of different points in the mechanism to understand their motion and interactions. (Graphical method)
\(\Rightarrow\) Combination of Springs (Video) When springs are combined, their equivalent spring constant or stiffness can be determined based on whether they are in series or parallel. Springs in Series: \(\frac{1}{S_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{S_1} + \frac{1}{S_2}\)
Springs in Parallel: \(S_{\text{eq}} = S_1 + S_2\)
Cutting of Springs The cutting of springs refers to the process of altering the length or number of coils in a spring to adjust its stiffness or spring constant. Cutting in ration m:n
\(S_1=\frac{m+n}{m}\)&\(S_2=\frac{m+n}{n}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Cycloidal Motion Expression The expression for cycloidal motion describes the displacement \(x\) of a point on a cycloidal path in terms of the parameter \(\theta\), with \(S\) as the amplitude and \(\theta_0\) as the period. \(x = S\left(\frac{\theta}{\theta_0} - \frac{1}{2\pi}\sin\left(\frac{2\pi \theta}{\theta_0}\right)\right)\)
