\(\Rightarrow\) Complex stresses
Stresses on Oblique plane (Video 1) (Video 2) The stresses on an oblique plane in a two-dimensional stress state are given by the normal stress (\(\sigma_{\theta}\)) and the shear stress (\(\tau_{\theta}\)). They depend on the normal stresses (\(\sigma_x\) and \(\sigma_y\)) acting on the plane, as well as the shear stress (\(\tau_{xy}\)) along the plane. \(\sigma_{\theta} = \frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}+\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\cos{2\theta}+\tau_{xy}\sin{2\theta}\)
\(\tau_{\theta} = -\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\sin{2\theta}+\tau_{xy}\cos{2\theta}\)
Mohr's circle (Video) Mohr's circle is a graphical representation of two-dimensional stress states. It is constructed by plotting the normal and shear stresses acting on a plane and helps in determining principal stresses, maximum shear stress, and stress transformations. Using the above two equations Mohr circle can be drawn
Orientation of Principal Planes (\(\theta_p\)) (Video) The orientation of principal planes in complex stresses can be determined using this formula. It calculates the angle between the principal planes and the x-axis based on the shear stress (\(\tau_{xy}\)) and the difference between the normal stresses (\(\sigma_x\) and \(\sigma_y\)). \(\theta_p=\frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{2\tau_{xy}}{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}\right)\)
Principal Stresses The principal stresses in complex stress states can be calculated using these formulas. These formulas determine the maximum and minimum normal stresses (\(\sigma_{1,2}\)) and the maximum shear stresses (\(\tau_{1,2}\)) in the complex stress state. \(\sigma_{1,2}=\left(\frac{\sigma_x+\sigma_y}{2}\right)\pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+(\tau_{xy})^2}\)
\(\tau_{1,2}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x-\sigma_y}{2}\right)^2+(\tau_{xy})^2}\)
Absolute Maximum shear stress The absolute maximum shear stress (\(\tau_{max}\)) in complex stress states can be determined by evaluating the maximum shear stress values from the three mutually perpendicular planes of stress. \(\tau_{max} = \text{maximum of } \tau_{12}, \tau_{23}, \tau_{31}\)
\(\quad = \text{maximum of } \left|\frac{\sigma_1-\sigma_2}{2}\right|, \left|\frac{\sigma_2-\sigma_3}{2}\right|, \left|\frac{\sigma_3-\sigma_1}{2}\right|\)
Bending & Torsion (Video) In combined loading scenarios involving bending and torsion, the formulas for the principal stresses and shear stresses are: \(\sigma_{1,2} = \frac{16}{\pi d^3}[M \pm \sqrt{M^2+T^2}]\)
\(\tau_{1,2} = \left|\frac{\sigma_1-\sigma_2}{2}\right| = \frac{16}{\pi d^3}\sqrt{M^2+T^2}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Hardness tester (A hardness tester is an instrument used to determine the hardness of a material, which relates to its resistance to indentation or scratching.) (Images)
Rockwell Hardness Tester The Rockwell hardness test measures the depth of indentation caused by a specified load. It uses a diamond or a ball indenter and applies an initial minor load, followed by a major load. The hardness value is determined based on the difference in depth before and after applying the major load. Rockwell hardness values are expressed as a combination of a hardness scale (e.g., HRC, HRB) and a numerical value.
Brinell Hardness Tester The Brinell hardness test involves indenting a spherical hardened steel ball into the material under a specified load. The diameter of the indentation is measured, and the hardness value is calculated by dividing the load applied by the surface area of the indentation. Brinell hardness values are expressed in HB units.
Vickers Hardness Tester The Vickers hardness test uses a diamond pyramid-shaped indenter. A load is applied, and the diagonal lengths of the resulting indentation are measured. The hardness value is calculated by dividing the load applied by the surface area of the indentation. Vickers hardness values are expressed in HV units.

\(\Rightarrow\) Bending equation (Video) The bending equation relates the bending moment (M) to the moment of inertia (I) and the stress (\(\sigma\)) with respect to the centroidal axis and the distance (y) from the centroidal axis. It also relates the Young's modulus (E) to the radius of curvature (R). \(\frac{M}{I} = \frac{\sigma}{y} = \frac{E}{R}\)
Section Modulus Section modulus is a geometric property of a shape's cross-section. It measures the ability of a given section to resist bending. \(Z=\frac{I}{y}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Torsion equation (Video) The torsion equation relates various parameters in torsional loading of structural members. It describes the relationship between torque (\(T\)), polar moment of inertia (\(J\)), shear stress (\(\tau\)), radius (\(R\)), shear modulus (\(G\)), twist angle (\(\theta\)), and length (\(L\)). \(\frac{T}{J} = \frac{\tau}{R} = \frac{G\theta}{L}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Shear Stress in beam (Video) The stress experienced by a material subjected to a shearing force (\(F_s\)) perpendicular to the cross-sectional area (\(A\)) at a distance (\(\bar y\)) from the neutral axis. \(\tau = \frac{F_s (A\bar y)}{Ib}\) (Emperical)
For Rectangular cross-section \(\tau=\frac{3}{2}\tau_{avg}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Emperical Relations Emperical relationships between different elastic moduli (Young's modulus, bulk modulus, and shear modulus) and Poisson's ratio. Elastic modulus and bulk modulus \(\Rightarrow E=3K(1-2\mu)\)
Elastic modulus and shear modulus \(\Rightarrow E=2G(1+\mu)\)
Elastic modulus, bulk modulus, and shear modulus \(\Rightarrow E=\frac{9KG}{G+3K}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Moment-Area Method The moment-area method is a structural analysis technique used to determine displacements and slopes in beams subjected to external loads. It involves calculating the area under the bending moment diagram to find the desired quantities. \(\theta_A -\theta_B = \frac{\text{Area under BMD between Aand B}}{EI}\)
\(y = \text{First moment of area under BMD between Aand B}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Deflection of Beam The deflection of a simply supported beam can be calculated using the formula, \(y_{max} = \frac{WL^3}{48EI}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Strain Energy Method for Deflection of Beam (Video) The strain energy method is used to calculate the deflection of a beam by integrating the square of the bending moment over the length of the beam, divided by the product of the modulus of elasticity and moment of inertia. \(U = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^L \frac{M^2}{EI} dx\)
