α-Ferrite A solid solution of carbon in iron with a body-centered cubic crystal structure. α-Ferrite is the stable form of iron below 912°C, and it has a maximum solubility of 0.022% carbon.
Austenite A solid solution of carbon in iron with a face-centered cubic crystal structure. Austenite is the stable form of iron between 912°C and 1394°C, and it has a maximum solubility of 2.11% carbon.
δ-Ferrite A solid solution of carbon in iron with a body-centered cubic crystal structure. δ-Ferrite is the stable form of iron above 1394°C, and it has a maximum solubility of 0.09% carbon.
Cementite A compound of iron and carbon with a fixed composition of 6.67% carbon. Cementite is a hard, brittle material that forms when iron and carbon combine at a fixed ratio.
Pearlite A lamellar mixture of ferrite and cementite formed by the eutectoid reaction. Pearlite is a common microstructure in steels and consists of alternating layers of ferrite and cementite.
Ladeburite A mixture of austenite and cementite formed by the eutectic reaction. Ladeburite is a two-phase mixture that forms in the iron-carbon system when the composition is at the eutectic point.
Eutectic reaction A reaction in which a liquid phase transforms into two solid phases of different compositions simultaneously and at a constant temperature. The eutectic reaction occurs at a specific composition and temperature, and it produces a two-phase mixture.

\(\Rightarrow\) Heat treatment processes (A group of processes used to alter the properties of materials by heating and cooling them in a controlled manner.) (Video)
Annealing A heat treatment process that involves heating a material to a high temperature and then cooling it slowly to make it softer and more ductile.
Normalizing A heat treatment process that involves heating a material to a high temperature and then cooling it in air.
Austempering A heat treatment process that involves quenching a material in a bath of molten salt or lead to obtain a bainitic microstructure.
Tempering A heat treatment process that involves heating a material to a temperature below its critical temperature and then cooling it.
Quenching A heat treatment process that involves rapidly cooling a material to room temperature to obtain a martensitic microstructure.
\(\Rightarrow\) Effect of alloying elements in HSS The addition of Vanadium to high-speed-steel (HSS) serves to remove slag impurities, reduce nitrogen levels, and increase cutting efficiency. Vanadium promotes the formation of hard carbides, enhancing the wear resistance of HSS tools.
\(\Rightarrow\) Crystal Defects
Twin A twin is a crystal defect where a portion of a crystal exhibits mirror image or rotational symmetry with respect to another portion, resulting from a structural transformation within the crystal lattice induced by external factors like stress or temperature changes.
Stacking fault A stacking fault is a region of disorder within a crystal lattice where the regular stacking sequence of atomic planes is interrupted, resulting from a deviation or displacement of atomic planes in the crystal structure.
Anti-phase boundary An anti-phase boundary (APB) is a planar defect that occurs at the interface between two regions of a crystal with a slight difference in crystallographic orientation or atomic arrangement, characterized by a phase shift or misalignment in the stacking sequence of atoms across the boundary.
Orowan loop The Orowan loop is a mechanism that describes the movement of dislocations in a crystal lattice. When a dislocation moves through the crystal, it leaves behind a trail of lattice distortion called the Orowan loop.
\(\Rightarrow\) Griffith's crack theory Griffith's crack theory states that the concentrated stress (\(S_c\)) at the crack tip is related to the applied stress (\(S\)), crack length (\(c\)), and the radius of curvature (\(r\)). It describes the conditions for crack propagation and fracture in brittle materials. \(S_c = 2S \sqrt{\frac{c}{r}}\)
