\(\Rightarrow\) Standard values
For Air Cp represents the specific heat capacity at constant pressure. It is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one unit mass by one degree Celsius at constant pressure. \(C_p = 1 \, \text{kJ/kgK}\)
Cv represents the specific heat capacity of air at constant volume. It is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one unit mass by one degree Celsius at constant volume. \(C_v = 0.718 \, \text{kJ/kgK}\)
The specific gas constant represents the ratio of the universal gas constant to the molar mass of a particular gas. It relates the pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas. \(R = 0.287 \, \text{kJ/kgK}\)
Universal gas constant (\(\bar R\)) The universal gas constant represents the constant of proportionality in the ideal gas law. It relates the pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of substance of any gas. \(\bar R = 8.314 \, \text{kJ/kgK}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Mayer's Formula (Video) This equation relates the specific heat capacities at constant pressure (\(C_p\)) and constant volume (\(C_v\)) to the gas constant (\(R\)). It represents the relationship between the thermodynamic properties of a gas. \(C_p - C_v = R\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Poisson's law (Video) A law that relates the pressure, volume, and specific heat ratio of a gas at constant temperature. \(PV^{\gamma} = \text{constant}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) \(\frac{C_p}{C_v} = \gamma\) (Video) This equation establishes the relationship between the specific heat capacities at constant pressure (\(C_p\)) and constant volume (\(C_v\)) and the heat capacity ratio (\(\gamma\)). It is a key property of an ideal gas. \(\frac{C_p}{C_v} = \gamma\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Adiabatic work done (Video) The adiabatic work done (\(W_{\text{adiabatic}}\)) is given by \(W_{\text{adiabatic}} = \frac{P_1V_1 - P_2V_2}{\gamma -1}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Thermodynamic Relations (Video)
Maxwell Equations (Video) Maxwell equation (1):
\(Tds=du+Pdv \Rightarrow \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_s=\left(\frac{-\partial P}{\partial s}\right)_v\)
Maxwell equation (2):
\(Tds=dh-vdP \Rightarrow \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_s=\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial s}\right)_p\)
Maxwell equation (3):
\(dg=dh-Tds-sdT \Rightarrow \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial T}\right)_p=\left(-\frac{\partial s}{\partial P}\right)_T\)
Maxwell equation (4):
\(df=du-Tds-sdT \Rightarrow \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_v=\left(\frac{\partial s}{\partial v}\right)_T\)
TdS Equations (Video) The TdS equations relate changes in temperature, pressure, and volume to changes in entropy in thermodynamics. \(TdS=C_vdT+T\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_v dv\)
\(TdS=C_pdT-T\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_p dP\)
Joule-Thomson Expansion or Joule-Kelvin Effect The Joule-Thomson expansion, also known as the Joule-Kelvin effect, describes the process of a gas flowing through a throttle valve or porous plug while undergoing an isenthalpic (constant enthalpy) process. \(\Delta T = \frac{\mu}{C_p}\left(T \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_H - P\right)\)......(Not Varified)
Clausius-Clapeyron Equation The Clausius-Clapeyron equation relates the vapor pressure of a substance at one temperature to its vapor pressure at another temperature, based on the enthalpy difference and specific volume difference between the two states. \(\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_{\text{sat}} = \frac{h_g - h_f}{T_{\text{sat}}\left(v_g - v_f\right)}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Coefficient of Volumetric Expansion (\(\beta\)) The coefficient of volumetric expansion represents the fractional change in volume per unit change in temperature of a substance. \(\beta=\frac{1}{V}\left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial T}\right)_p\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Loss of Available Energy \(Loss\ of\ Available\ energy = T_0(ds_2-ds_1)\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Maximum Work Output The condition for maximum work output in a heat engine is that the temperature of the sink (\(T_2\)) is the geometric mean of the temperatures of the source (\(T_1\)) and the working substance (\(T_3\)). \(T_2=\sqrt{T_1T_3}\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Isentropic Process An isentropic process is an idealized thermodynamic process that occurs without any entropy change. \(\frac{T_2}{T_1} = \left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right)^{\gamma-1} = \left(\frac{P_2}{P_1}\right)^{\frac{\gamma-1}{\gamma}}\)
