Runge-Kutta Methods\(\quad\)......(Memorize)
The basic idea of the Runge-Kutta method is to approximate the solution of an ODE by evaluating the derivatives at different points within a small time step and then using a weighted average to update the solution to the next time step.		 First-Order Runge-Kutta (RK1) Method:
\(y_{i+1} = y_i + k_1\)
\(k_1 = h \cdot f(x_i, y_i)\)
Second-Order Runge-Kutta (RK2) Method:
\(y_{i+1} = y_i + \frac{1}{2}(k_1+k_2)\)
\(k_1 = h \cdot f(x_i, y_i)\)
\(k_2 = h \cdot f(x_i + \frac{h}{2}, y_i + \frac{k_1}{2})\)		 Third-Order Runge-Kutta (RK3) Method:
\(y_{i+1} = y_i + \frac{1}{6}(k_1 + 4k_2 + k_3)\)
\(k_1 = h \cdot f(x_i, y_i)\)
\(k_2 = h \cdot f(x_i + \frac{h}{2}, y_i + \frac{k_1}{2})\)
\(k_3 = h \cdot f(x_i + h, y_i - k_1 + 2k_2)\)
Fourth-Order Runge-Kutta (RK4) Method:
\(y_{i+1} = y_i + \frac{1}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4)\)
\(k_1 = h \cdot f(x_i, y_i)\)
\(k_2 = h \cdot f(x_i + \frac{h}{2}, y_i + \frac{k_1}{2})\)
\(k_3 = h \cdot f(x_i + \frac{h}{2}, y_i + \frac{k_2}{2})\)
\(k_4 = h \cdot f(x_i + h, y_i + k_3)\)

\(\Rightarrow\) Fourier series
Fourier series A representation of a periodic function as an infinite sum of sine and cosine functions (or complex exponentials), allowing the function to be expressed in terms of its frequency components. \(f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)\)
The coefficients \(a_0\), \(a_n\), and \(b_n\) for a Fourier series of a function f(x) are given by: \(a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)dx\)
\(a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\cos(nx)dx\)
\(b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\sin(nx)dx\)

\(\Rightarrow\) Harmonic function A function that satisfies Laplace's equation, meaning its second partial derivatives with respect to the independent variables sum up to zero. Harmonic functions often arise in physical and engineering applications. If \(u(x, y)\) is a harmonic function, it satisfies Laplace's equation: \(u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0\)

\(\Rightarrow\) Inclusion-Exclusion Principle (Wikipedia) A principle used in combinatorics to count the number of elements in the union of multiple sets. It states that to calculate the total count, we need to include the elements in each set, exclude the elements common to two sets, include the elements common to three sets, and so on. \(|A \cup B \cup C| = |A| + |B| + |C| - |A \cap B| - |B \cap C| - |C \cap A| + |A \cap B \cap C|\)
\(\Rightarrow\) Contour Integral (Residue Theorem) A contour integral of the form \(\oint_{\Gamma} F(z) \, dz\) can be evaluated using the Residue Theorem, which states that the value of the integral around a closed path \(\Gamma\) enclosing an isolated singularity is \(2\pi i\) times the residue of the function at that singularity. \(\text{Res}(f, a) = \lim_{{z \to a}} (z - a) \cdot F(z)\)
\(\oint_{\Gamma} F(z) \, dz = 2\pi i \left(\text{Res}(f, a_1) + \text{Res}(f, a_2)\right) + \ldots\) (Residue Theorem)
